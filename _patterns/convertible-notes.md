# Convertible Notes

### 1. Overview

A convertible note, also known as convertible debt or a convertible loan, is a form of short-term debt that converts into equity in a future financing round. It is a hybrid instrument that combines features of both debt and equity, offering a flexible financing solution for early-stage startups. The core purpose of a convertible note is to defer the complex and often contentious process of valuing a young company until a later stage when there are more data points to inform a fair valuation. This allows startups to secure seed funding quickly and efficiently, without getting bogged down in premature valuation negotiations with investors. The problem it solves is the classic chicken-and-egg dilemma of early-stage fundraising: investors want to see traction before committing to a valuation, but startups need funding to achieve that traction. By using a convertible note, both parties can agree to postpone the valuation discussion while still allowing the startup to access the capital it needs to grow.

The concept of convertible debt has been around for a long time, but its application in the context of startup financing was popularized in the early 2000s by angel investors and venture capitalists in Silicon Valley. Prominent figures like Paul Graham of Y Combinator played a significant role in advocating for the use of simpler, founder-friendly financing instruments like convertible notes and, later, the SAFE (Simple Agreement for Future Equity). The goal was to streamline the seed funding process, reduce legal costs, and allow founders to focus on building their businesses rather than on complex financial engineering. In relation to commons-aligned value creation, convertible notes can be a double-edged sword. On one hand, they can provide a more accessible and less dilutive form of initial funding for commons-oriented projects that might struggle to attract traditional venture capital. On the other hand, the eventual conversion to equity can introduce a tension between the profit-maximizing incentives of shareholders and the broader social or ecological mission of the commons.

### 2. Core Principles

1.  **Deferred Valuation:** The fundamental principle of a convertible note is the postponement of a company's valuation until a future priced equity round. This allows the company to raise capital without having to agree on a specific valuation at a very early stage, which can be difficult and contentious.

2.  **Debt with an Equity Option:** A convertible note is legally a debt instrument, with a stated interest rate and maturity date. However, its primary purpose is not to be repaid as a loan, but to convert into equity at a later date, giving the investor a stake in the company.

3.  **Investor Protection and Upside:** To compensate investors for the additional risk of investing early, convertible notes typically include a valuation cap and/or a discount. The valuation cap sets a maximum valuation at which the note will convert, protecting the investor from excessive dilution if the company's valuation skyrockets. The discount allows the investor to convert their debt into equity at a lower price than later-stage investors.

4.  **Simplicity and Speed:** Compared to a priced equity round, a convertible note financing is generally simpler, faster, and less expensive to execute. The legal documentation is more standardized, and there is less negotiation involved, allowing startups to close their funding rounds more quickly.

5.  **Alignment of Interests:** By converting into equity, the interests of the convertible note holders become aligned with those of the company's founders and other equity holders. Everyone is incentivized to work towards increasing the long-term value of the company.

### 3. Key Practices

1.  **Negotiating Key Terms:** The most critical practice in a convertible note financing is the negotiation of the key terms: the valuation cap, the discount rate, the interest rate, and the maturity date. These terms will determine the investor's ultimate return and the level of dilution for the founders.

2.  **Defining the Qualifying Financing Round:** The convertible note agreement must clearly define what constitutes a "qualifying financing round" that will trigger the conversion of the notes. This typically includes a minimum amount of new investment to be raised in the round.

3.  **Handling the Maturity Date:** The agreement should specify what happens if the company does not raise a qualifying financing round before the maturity date. The noteholders may have the option to convert the notes into equity at a pre-agreed valuation, or the company may be required to repay the loan with accrued interest.

4.  **Managing the Cap Table:** Startups must carefully track all of their convertible notes and understand how they will impact the company's capitalization table upon conversion. This is crucial for managing dilution and for providing transparency to future investors.

5.  **Considering the Pro-Rata Rights:** Some convertible notes may include pro-rata rights, which give the investor the right to participate in future financing rounds to maintain their ownership percentage. This can be a valuable right for investors, but it can also lead to greater dilution for founders.

6.  **Using Standardized Documents:** To save time and legal fees, it is a common practice to use standardized convertible note documents, such as those provided by Y Combinator or other reputable sources. However, it is still essential to have the documents reviewed by a lawyer to ensure they are appropriate for the specific circumstances.

### 4. Implementation

Implementing a convertible note financing involves a series of steps, starting with the preparation of a term sheet that outlines the key terms of the investment. Once the term sheet is agreed upon by both the company and the investors, the next step is to draft the legal documents, which typically include a convertible promissory note and a note purchase agreement. These documents should be prepared by an experienced startup lawyer to ensure they are legally sound and protect the interests of all parties. After the documents are finalized and signed, the investors will wire the funds to the company, and the financing round is officially closed. A key consideration during this process is to ensure that all securities laws and regulations are complied with, which may involve filing certain notices with the relevant authorities.

Real-world examples of companies that have successfully used convertible notes in their early stages include some of the biggest names in the tech industry, such as Airbnb, Dropbox, and Uber. These companies used convertible notes to raise their initial seed funding, which allowed them to build their products, gain traction, and eventually raise much larger priced equity rounds at significantly higher valuations. For commons-aligned projects, the implementation of convertible notes requires a careful consideration of how the eventual conversion to equity will impact the project's mission and governance. It may be necessary to incorporate additional provisions into the convertible note agreement to protect the commons-oriented aspects of the project, such as by creating a multi-stakeholder governance structure or by linking the conversion to the achievement of certain social or environmental milestones.

### 6. When to Use

*   When a startup needs to raise seed funding quickly and efficiently.
*   When the founders and investors cannot agree on a valuation for the company.
*   When the company wants to avoid the legal costs and complexity of a priced equity round.
*   When the company is in a strong negotiating position and can secure favorable terms.
*   When the company has a clear path to raising a priced equity round in the near future.

### 7. Anti-Patterns and Gotchas

*   **Uncapped Notes:** An uncapped convertible note, which does not have a valuation cap, can be very unfavorable to investors, as their investment will be diluted significantly if the company's valuation increases dramatically.
*   **High Valuation Caps:** A valuation cap that is set too high can also be detrimental to investors, as it will limit their potential upside.
*   **Multiple Convertible Note Rounds:** Raising multiple rounds of convertible notes can create a complex and messy capitalization table, which can be a red flag for future investors.
*   **Ignoring the Maturity Date:** Failing to plan for the maturity date of the convertible notes can put the company in a difficult position if it is unable to raise a qualifying financing round in time.
*   **Not Understanding the Dilution:** Founders must have a clear understanding of how the convertible notes will dilute their ownership stake in the company upon conversion.
