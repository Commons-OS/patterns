### 1. Overview

An 83(b) election is a provision within the United States Internal Revenue Code (IRC) that allows founders, employees, and other service providers of a company to accelerate their income tax liability on equity compensation that is subject to vesting. The core purpose of this election is to pay taxes on the fair market value of the restricted stock at the time it is granted, rather than at the time it vests. This is particularly relevant in the context of early-stage startups, where the value of the stock at the time of grant is typically very low, often at its par value. By making an 83(b) election, the individual is choosing to be taxed on this low initial value, and any subsequent appreciation in the stock's value is treated as a capital gain, which is generally taxed at a lower rate than ordinary income. This can result in significant tax savings if the company's valuation increases substantially over time.

The problem that the 83(b) election solves is the potential for a large and often illiquid tax burden on individuals receiving equity compensation. Without this election, the recipient would be taxed on the fair market value of the stock as it vests. In a successful startup, the stock's value can increase dramatically between the grant date and the vesting dates. This means the individual would face a growing tax liability with each vesting event, a liability that must be paid in cash even though the stock itself is often illiquid and cannot be easily sold to cover the tax bill. This can create a significant financial strain, sometimes referred to as "golden handcuffs," where the recipient is tied to the company but struggles to afford the taxes on their equity. The 83(b) election, a feature of the U.S. tax code, was designed to mitigate this issue by allowing the tax event to occur when the stock's value is minimal.

While not a business pattern in the traditional sense, the 83(b) election is a critical financial and legal maneuver that has become a standard practice in the startup world, particularly in the United States. Its origin lies within Section 83 of the Internal Revenue Code, which governs the taxation of property transferred in connection with the performance of services. The popularization of this election is a direct result of the growth of the venture-backed startup ecosystem, where equity compensation is a primary means of attracting and retaining talent. In the context of commons-aligned value creation, the 83(b) election can be seen as a tool that, while not directly promoting commons principles, can indirectly support them. By reducing the personal financial risk for founders and early employees, it can enable them to focus on building long-term, sustainable value rather than being driven by short-term financial pressures. This can create a more stable and committed team, which is essential for any organization, including those with a commons-oriented mission. It allows for a more equitable distribution of the potential upside of a venture, aligning the financial interests of the team with the long-term success of the enterprise.

### 2. Core Principles

1.  **Taxation at Grant, Not Vesting:** The fundamental principle of the 83(b) election is the choice to be taxed on the value of restricted stock at the time it is granted, rather than when it vests. This shifts the tax event to the earliest possible moment, when the stock's value is typically at its lowest.

2.  **Locking in the Cost Basis:** By electing to be taxed at the grant date, the recipient establishes their cost basis in the stock at its fair market value on that date. This is crucial for determining future capital gains.

3.  **Conversion of Future Appreciation to Capital Gains:** Any increase in the stock's value after the grant date is treated as a capital gain, which is generally taxed at a more favorable rate than ordinary income. This is the primary driver of the potential tax savings offered by the 83(b) election.

4.  **Assumption of Risk:** The individual making the election is betting on the future success of the company. If the company fails or the stock's value declines, the taxes paid at the time of the election are not refundable. This represents a calculated risk on the part of the recipient.

5.  **Time-Sensitive and Irrevocable:** The 83(b) election must be filed with the IRS within 30 days of the stock grant. This is a strict deadline, and the election is irrevocable once made. This underscores the importance of timely and informed decision-making.

6.  **Applicability to Restricted Property:** The 83(b) election applies specifically to property, most commonly restricted stock, that is transferred in connection with the performance of services and is subject to a substantial risk of forfeiture (e.g., vesting). It does not apply to all forms of equity compensation, such as stock options, until they are exercised.

### 3. Key Practices

1.  **Immediate Assessment upon Grant:** As soon as restricted stock is granted, the recipient, in consultation with legal and tax advisors, should immediately assess whether an 83(b) election is appropriate. The 30-day window for filing is short, so prompt action is critical.

2.  **Accurate Determination of Fair Market Value (FMV):** The 83(b) election requires reporting the fair market value of the stock at the time of grant. For early-stage companies, this is often a nominal amount, but it must be a reasonable and defensible valuation to avoid future challenges from the IRS.

3.  **Proper and Timely Filing:** The 83(b) election form must be completed accurately and mailed to the appropriate IRS service center via certified mail with a return receipt requested. This provides proof of timely filing. A copy of the election should also be provided to the company and retained by the individual.

4.  **Inclusion with Tax Return:** A copy of the 83(b) election must be attached to the individual's federal income tax return for the year in which the stock was granted. This is a crucial step to ensure the IRS has a complete record of the election.

5.  **Payment of Upfront Taxes:** The individual must be prepared to pay the income tax due on the fair market value of the stock at the time of the grant. While this is often a small amount for early-stage companies, it is a necessary part of the process.

6.  **Understanding the Forfeiture Risk:** The individual must fully understand that if they leave the company before the stock vests, they will forfeit the stock and will not be able to recover the taxes they paid as a result of the 83(b) election. This is a key risk to consider.

7.  **Consultation with Professionals:** Given the complexity of tax law and the potential financial consequences, it is essential for individuals to consult with experienced legal and tax professionals before making an 83(b) election. These professionals can provide personalized advice based on the individual's specific circumstances.

8.  **Company Support and Education:** Startups should proactively educate their employees and founders about the 83(b) election and provide them with the necessary documentation and support to make the filing process as smooth as possible. This can include providing template forms and clear instructions.

### 4. Implementation

Implementing an 83(b) election is a time-sensitive and precise process. The first step is to recognize when an 83(b) election is possible and potentially beneficial. This occurs when an individual receives property, such as restricted stock, in exchange for services, and that property is subject to a vesting schedule. Once the grant has been made, the 30-day clock for filing the election begins. The individual must then prepare the 83(b) election form, which is a letter to the IRS. This letter must include the individual's name, address, and taxpayer identification number; a description of the property (e.g., the number of shares of stock); the date the property was transferred; the taxable year for which the election is being made; and any restrictions on the property. The fair market value of the property at the time of transfer must also be stated, along with the amount, if any, paid for the property. A real-world example would be a founder of a new C-corporation who is granted 1,000,000 shares of restricted stock at a par value of $0.00001 per share. The founder would pay $10 for the stock and file an 83(b) election, reporting $10 of ordinary income. If the company is later valued at $10 million, the founder's stock would be worth $1 million, and this appreciation would be taxed as a capital gain upon sale, not as ordinary income as it vests.

After preparing the election form, it must be signed and dated, and then sent to the IRS service center where the individual files their taxes. It is highly recommended to send the form via certified mail with a return receipt requested to have proof of the filing date. A copy of the election form should also be delivered to the company, and the individual should retain a copy for their own records. Finally, a copy of the 83(b) election must be attached to the individual's federal income tax return for the year in which the property was received. Key considerations during this process include the accuracy of the fair market value determination, the strict 30-day deadline, and the irrevocable nature of the election. It is also crucial to understand the financial implications, including the upfront tax payment and the risk of forfeiture. For instance, if an early employee at a tech startup receives restricted stock and files an 83(b) election, but then leaves the company before the stock fully vests, they will have paid taxes on stock they ultimately did not receive, and those taxes are not refundable.

In practice, most startup law firms and equity management platforms like Carta provide templates and guidance for making an 83(b) election. Companies often include a pre-filled 83(b) election form as part of the onboarding paperwork for new hires who receive restricted stock. This helps to streamline the process and reduce the risk of errors. However, it is still the individual's responsibility to ensure the form is filed correctly and on time. The decision to make an 83(b) election should always be made in consultation with a qualified tax advisor who can assess the individual's specific situation and risk tolerance. The advisor can help to weigh the potential tax savings against the risk of forfeiture and the possibility that the company's stock may not appreciate as expected. This professional guidance is essential for making an informed decision that aligns with the individual's long-term financial goals.

### 6. When to Use

The 83(b) election is a strategic decision that is most potent in specific contexts. It is particularly advantageous for **early-stage startups** where the fair market value of the stock is at its nadir, often equivalent to its par value. This timing is crucial as it minimizes the upfront tax liability for the individual. The strategy is also most effective for companies with **high-growth potential**, as the primary benefit of the election is to convert future appreciation into capital gains. The greater the anticipated increase in the stock's value, the more substantial the potential tax savings become. The individuals who stand to benefit most from this election are **founders and early employees** who receive significant equity grants with vesting schedules. For them, the 83(b) election is a mechanism to establish a low cost basis for their equity, thereby maximizing their potential future returns. It is important to remember that this election is specifically for **restricted stock** or other property subject to vesting; it is not applicable to stock options until they are exercised. Furthermore, the decision to file an 83(b) election should be aligned with a **long-term commitment** to the company. The individual should have a strong conviction in the company's future success and be prepared to remain with the company throughout their vesting period. Finally, it is essential to note that the 83(b) election is a provision of the **U.S. Internal Revenue Code** and is therefore only available to U.S. taxpayers.

### 7. Anti-Patterns and Gotchas

While the 83(b) election offers significant advantages, there are several common pitfalls and "gotchas" that can undermine its effectiveness. The most critical of these is **missing the 30-day filing deadline**. This is a strict and non-negotiable timeframe, and failure to adhere to it results in the permanent loss of the opportunity to make the election. Another common error is **incorrect filing**. This can include sending the election to the wrong IRS service center, failing to use certified mail to obtain proof of filing, or omitting required information from the election form, any of which can invalidate the election. Furthermore, a frequent oversight is **forgetting to include a copy of the election with the individual's tax return** for the year of the grant. This can lead to unnecessary complications and inquiries from the IRS. A more strategic error is **ignoring professional advice**. The 83(b) election is a complex tax matter with significant financial implications, and proceeding without the guidance of experienced legal and tax professionals is a risky proposition. Individuals must also not **underestimate the forfeiture risk**. It is crucial to be realistic about the possibility of departing from the company before the stock fully vests, as the taxes paid on unvested, forfeited stock are non-refundable. Lastly, while a low valuation is advantageous, an **overly optimistic or indefensible valuation** can be challenged by the IRS, potentially leading to back taxes and penalties.
